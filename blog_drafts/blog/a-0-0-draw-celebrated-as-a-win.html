The Trophy Ceremony for a 0-0 Draw

The "Successful" Release

Once I was contracting for a team who were under pressure to get a production release out by a certain deadline.

We just managed to succeed and the team celebrated, the "champagne" was out and there were backslaps aplenty.

But something didn't quite feel right for me. I knew that in order to reach the deadline, we had dropped certain features late on, narrowed the scope and not fixed all the bugs.

Everybody around me seemed to be happy and proud to have delivered on time. And that dissonance got me wondering:

How can two groups of professionals look at the same outcome and see total opposites?

The answer often lies in perspective and in what success looks like within a given environment.

When a 0-0 Draw Feels Like a Win

In technology teams, success is not always defined in the same way.

In some environments, shipping on time is the primary goal. When deadlines are tight and the pressure is high, delivery becomes the focal point. Features go out, deadlines are met and that is rightly recognised.

But there can often be a subtle trade-off.

Craft, robustness and long-term quality are harder to see in the moment. They are quieter and less visible, so they can sometimes take a back seat to immediate outcomes.

Over time, this can create the dynamic where delivery is the achievement, even when there are known compromises.

And in that context, the 0-0 draw feels like a win.

Incentives At Play

This is rarely about individuals. Most engineers care about doing good work. But people respond to the incentives around them.

If the reward and acclaim are tied in with shipping on time, the team will focus solely on that aim. The system imperceptibly reinforces the behaviour it values most.

If today's result is the glory, why spend time developing strategies, patterns of play and efficient systems when they can just lump it long to the big man?

Different Environments, Different Priorities

Not all teams operate under the same constraints.

In some environments, there is more space to think longer-term. Effort can be front-loaded to reduce future issues. There is time to design, test and refine.

In these settings, success is often defined differently. Shipping still matters, but so does what happens afterwards:

  • Does the system hold up over time? Next week, next month, next year?
  • Is it easy to work with and extend?
  • Are problems being reduced or repeated?

Processes tend to reflect this approach. Work is more structured, decisions are documented and quality is baked in rather than inspected later.

Discussions are encouraged. Questions are valued. The goal is not just to deliver but to deliver in a way that tomorrow's effort is reduced.

In these environments a 0-0 draw is not quite enough. The expectation is not just to finish the match but to improve how the game is played.

The Dilemma

These differences are not about good teams and bad teams. They are about team context, constraints, maturity and priorities.

Some environments are optimised for speed and delivery. Others are optimised for longevity and scale. Most sit somewhere in between.

And importantly, these characteristics are rarely shaped by individuals alone. They emerge from leadership priorities, organisational pressure and the systems that are in place.

Because of that, it can be difficult for a single person to significantly shift how an environment operates without broader alignment.

What This Means For You

Over time, you may start to notice a gap between the standards you value and the way your environment operates.

That's not necessarily a problem. But it's worth paying attention to.

The question becomes less about changing the environment on your own and more about understanding it clearly:

  • What does this team optimise for?
  • What gets rewarded?
  • What trade-offs are being made?

With that knowledge you can make a more informed decision about where you want to invest your time and energy.

For some, that means leaning into the environment and thriving within it. For others, it means gradually building evidence of the way they think and the standards they value.

That might be through a portfolio, writing or contributions outside of day-to-day work. Signals that help others understand how you approach problems.

Closing

Every team defines success in its own way.

What matters is being clear on what that definition is and whether it aligns with your own.

Because over time, what you celebrate shapes how you work. And that, more than anything determines the kind of systems you build.
