The Bone Specialist

The Curse of Knowledge

I remember being in a team meeting some time ago and listening to a colleague walk through an intricate problem. It was an extremely technical monologue and I vividly recall only really following it at a surface level. The rest of the room was leaning in and nodding along.

It wasn't that I didn't understand. It was more that something felt slightly incomplete. My instinct was that while the depth was impressive, we might be focussing on one part of the problem at the expense of the whole. This was the moment I started thinking about two different modes of expertise: one that goes impressively deeply into a single component and another that looks across how everything connects.

The Two Types of Expert

Traits Of A Bone Specialist

The bone specialist is deeply knowledgeable in a specific area. Their world is an isolated bone on a purple velvet tray. They understand density, permeability, colouring and mineral composition. They can tell you everything you need to know (and more) about the bone on the tray.

The language they use is precise and technical. They speak with confidence, grounded in their depth of knowledge.

They are often the go-to people when a problem sits within a single component. Their mode of thinking is super valuable when this is the case. However, when problems span multiple layers, this depth can sometimes make it harder to step back and see how that specific component fits into the wider system.

Traits Of A Chiropractor

Their world is broader than a Bone Specialist's but not as deeply knowledgeable in a specific area. They think in terms of the entire musculoskeletal system and focus on how the various muscles and bones interconnect.

They talk about purpose, gait, load, movement and interaction between components.

Because they hold more variables in mind, they can sometimes take longer to reach a conclusion. They are more likely to explore several possible causes before settling on a direction, recognising that a symptom showing up in one component could be caused by a problem in another.

The Data 'Bone Specialist'

Translating these two modes into the tech sphere, the Bone Specialist often shows up as someone who goes very deep into a particular tool or component. They understand stored procedures, configurations, coding language or specific layers of the stack in great detail.

When approaching problems, this can lead to solutions that are highly optimised within that layer. In a lot of cases that's exactly what's needed.

Complexity can arise when the problem spans multiple layers of a data system. In those situations, focusing too narrowly on one component can lead to solutions that work locally but don't fully address the wider context.

The Chiropractor Mindset

In contrast the Chiropractor takes a holistic view. Before touching anything they ask:

  • What are we trying to achieve here?
  • Where is the pain? (friction, inefficiency, complexity)
  • What is the current gait (how does the data flow from source)?
  • What does a healthy skeleton look like for this purpose? (what defines success?)

Only after assessing all this will the chiropractor roll up their sleeves. This information together with their view of the world will tell them where they need to work (could end up being multiple places) and what they need to do there. Their goal is not a perfect bone, but a pain-free, functioning system.

Promoting The Specialist

In many workplaces, deep technical expertise is highly visible. Detailed solutions, complex logic and confident delivery can be reassuring signals, particularly to those not working closely with the underlying systems.

As a result, this mode of working is often rewarded and promoted. It feels tangible and measurable.

In contrast, more system-orientated thinking can be less visible. These solutions tend to be simple and incremental rather than large, obvious outputs.

They can be perceived as slower or less decisive, even when the intent is to create more sustainable and adaptable solutions over time.

When Problems Are Approached From a Single Layer

Most teams (myself included at times) will naturally default to this mode under pressure. Some common signs are:

1. Conversations gravitate quickly to tools or components

The problem is not always framed holistically. Instead the conversation starts with the tooling. And either moves forward from there or becomes a deep-dive into the tool itself. And the problem statement gets lost.

2. "Discovery" happens mid-build

Important and influential aspects of the problem emerge during implementation rather than being explored upfront.

3. Projects can lack a clear skeleton

Work progresses through a series of detailed tasks without a clear overarching end-to-end view.

4. Increasing Complexity Over Time

Solutions become harder to reason about as more is added within a single layer.

Becoming a Chiropractor

This is a mode of thinking. The key is to always start from the user's point of view. What is their problem? What is their pain?

Sketch. Sketches are invaluable for understanding the helicopter view. I draw crude boxes and flow diagrams using pen and paper. I have also tried Microsoft Whiteboard and Excalidraw for this.

But draw with a purpose. Start with what the end state should look like. What is the best possible outcome for the user? Then move backwards through the layers asking "what has to happen here for that outcome?"

Talk like a human not a computer. For example use "getting answers faster" rather than "optimising the SQL stored procs".

When thinking about ways forward advocate for changes with small blast radii rather than jumping in with both feet. For example prototyping, spiking and phasing delivery. Chiropractors tend to iterate based on a loose framework, they don't rip everything up and start anew each time.

Rather than focussing on the characteristics of a bone, think about how bones fit together, what are the ways that one bone can influence another? It's not about deep domain knowledge about a bone but rather a fluent understanding of how the behaviour of one bone affects another bone in the skeleton.

Once a solution is found, evaluate it. Always be thinking "is there a simpler way?"

The Diagnosis

The industry has a tendency to value visible complexity and deep specialisation.

Both modes of thinking have their place. Deep expertise is essential but without an understanding of how components fit together, systems can become fragmented and harder to maintain over time.

Everything we build today shapes what we can build tomorrow. When solutions become unnecessarily complex, they can make future changes more difficult. It's like building a house on shaky foundations.

A useful question to keep coming back to is: is there a simpler way to achieve the same outcome?

Strong data systems balance depth with perspective. They respect the detail but are ultimately designed around how everything works together.
