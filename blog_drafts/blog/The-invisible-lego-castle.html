The Invisible Lego Castle

The Task

We were asked to build a castle out of Lego. One builder thought about the colour of the castle, the design of the walls and even included a drawbridge that moves and can actually be used - you can raise it and lower it. Every brick is snapped in perfectly to every other brick and the end result not only looks cool, it's fun to play with and....just works.

Another builder rushed in to build the minimal definition of a castle. They are always busy so they used whatever bricks they had and didn't consider putting in a drawbridge let alone one that raises and lowers.

Unfortunately in their rush (their context switching) they don't press the bricks together well enough and some of their turrets are wonky. But they talk the whole time they're doing the building so show engagement and attract attention. They also build some things on the side just for fun, like an ice cream shack. Things that have nothing to do with a castle.

So the first builder finishes their castle first and then gets an extra exercise. This time they have to build a shopping centre out of Lego.

Over time, the second builder's wonky castle starts leaning noticeably or in fact falls over in a big heap. And so they rush to fix it and work on it and get it stood up again. And management praises their efforts profusely.

Meanwhile not only is the first castle still standing, it's actually more secure and solid and more fun to play with. But no-one notices. Because it never falls down.

From Lego To Tech

This isn't about Lego, it's about how we build technology. Let me translate this metaphor into the language of software and data teams:

The Working Drawbridge

This represents going the extra mile: Building beyond the requirements to create a truly user-centric and robust artefact. Putting a smile on the end user's face.

The Wonky Bricks

This is technical debt, rushed code and a lack of thorough testing. A lack of true quality control and/or pride in the item. The feeling that "good enough" is good enough.

The Side Projects

This is the scope creep, the context switching which takes time and energy away from the task at hand. It's the small tasks that fall under "while I'm here I might as well do that too" which distract from the core goal.

The Collapse

This is the inevitable production bug or system failure as a result of the mediocre implementation.

The Rebuild and Praise

The "hero" culture of the arsonist turning into the fire-fighter where fixing a self-inflicted problem is rewarded more than preventing it in the first place.

Diagnosing The Disease

This disease, this issue isn't borne of malicious intent. People don't go to work to be villainous or vindictive. However they are a product of their culture and the incentives that are at play. In this team there were three main archetypes:

The Craftsperson

They are focussed on the end user. They want to produce something robust, elegant and high quality. Their success is a silent, stable system. And this feeling of doing it once and moving on.

The Performer

Their incentive is visibility and activity and being seen as the "go-to" problem solver. They subscribe to the "the squeaky wheel gets the grease" model and so are on broadcast a lot more than receiving. Their success is a dramatic narrative of rescue.

The Harmony-First Manager

They value team cohesion and visible effort above all else. This accidentally incentivises the creation of crises and drama within the team.

The Silent Cost

Drama is enticing to humans. There's a reason soap operas are so popular. But in a data team this skew between seemingly valuing the noisy, dramatic Performer rather than the quiet Craftsperson can have dire consequences for your team:

The Innovation Tax

The craftsperson's energy for building new, amazing things is drained by having to maintain the wonky castles. They stop building mechanical drawbridges because they don't have the mental bandwidth. The team's product offerings become that bit more average.

The Flight Risk

The team's most reliable builders will eventually leave. The system which rewards noisy crises and rework above innovation and robustness will burn them out. They will yearn for a team where they can be creative and valued for their creativity.

The Performance Of Mediocrity

If every castle collapse is treated as heroic rather than diagnostic, the team never learns. Collapses become par for the course. The team learns that without a collapse, a work item is not valued. The team never improves because the process doesn't learn from its mistakes or learn to avoid them, it just celebrates the repeats.

A Path Forward

The following is actionable advice for team leaders or managers wishing to create a culture which sees the invisible castles.

1. Look for the Quiet

Make an effort to spot and celebrate clean code, the robust data model, the lack of passbacks. This should be publicly celebrated. Not overly so but it should be made visible to communicate the standards expected.

2. Reward Prevention, Not Just Reaction

Consider how you allocate praise and what you praise. Does the bug-fixer get more recognition than the person whose code never has bugs?

3. Value The Drawbridge

Avoid punishing engineers for caring about robustness. Engineers should be encouraged to think about design, not just robotically develop.

4. Encourage Self Development

Protect time for developers to learn and develop themselves. Make it part of your role to discuss potential opportunities for growth, be it technical or leadership.

Conclusion

The next time you see a castle that never falls, I encourage you to take a closer look. There's probably a craftsperson behind it who's been building quietly for years. They don't need praise, they don't need limelight. But they do need to be seen.

A culture that only celebrates collapses eventually collapses itself.
