When "Just Ask" Starts To Hurt Productivity

Just Ask

Some time back I remember being in a team when we hired a new colleague. We worked for a software development company building pensions software for financial institutions. He came in at mid-level and as part of his probation, I hosted some training for him on a weekly basis to bring him up to speed.

He worked from home and so we did our best to ensure he felt included. As a new hire, I made sure he knew he could ask anything. Indeed me and a peer included him in a Teams group so he could contact either one of us at any time.

Over time it became clear that his first port of call often became asking one or both of us questions.

One morning, he popped up in the group chat with a question: "What does AFT stand for?".

We were trying to steer him to try to solve problems for himself first before asking for help. So we asked him "Have you Googled it with the word 'pension' after it?"

He hadn't. And when he did so, he got the answer he was after: "An Accounting for Tax (AFT) return is a mandatory, quarterly HMRC submission used by registered pension scheme administrators to report and pay tax charges."

He got the answer he wanted and thanked us profusely.

The problem: the path of least resistance (asking us) became the path of most resistance (two people having to context-switch each time).

The Hidden Cost of "Helping"

Helping colleagues is absolutely the right thing to do. It's how the team grow and develop together. However it is also a balancing act and I've observed that in some cases offering to help each time actually does the asker a dis-service long-term.

There are three main issues to be aware of:

The Context-Switching Tax

Every person in the team has their own workload and could be said to be busy. An interruption is often times more than just 2 minutes that's lost. It can actually be around 20 minutes of lost deep work momentum each time. Multiply that by several times a week and a number of hours can be spent weekly helping a colleague.

The Capability Tax

Every "just ask" moment prevents the asker from building critical problem-solving muscles. The tendency is to not want to waste time starting from scratch when it is easier to ask and share the thinking rather than it being developed independently.

We've all done it and it makes sense on a human level: it's the path of least resistance and the answer is obtained more quickly. In some circumstances, this can limit the team's opportunity to build collective problem-solving capability.

The Bus Factor

Many times the organisation becomes critically dependent on a few human search engines, creating a big risk. What happens if they are run over by a bus?

The Shift From Help Desk to Problem-Solvers

Please don't misunderstand me. I encourage engagement. New hires, juniors, heck even seniors should always be encouraged to be reach out for help. But it may help if we change the way in which this help is delivered.

Change the wording

Think about replacing the wording. Instead of "Just ask", try "Show me what you have tried".

This encourages the asker to explore the problem thoroughly before asking for help. This act itself can sometimes reduce the number of times that help has to be sought.

Additionally when the asker has to set the scene of the problem and explain what has already been tried, just the act of talking about it often leads them to be able to solve it for themselves. I've definitely fallen foul of this and can advocate for the benefits of explaining what you have done so far prior to asking someone else for help.

This leads to less interruption and allows more deep work to be carried out by the team. It also grows the asker's own problem-solving muscle rather than teaching them to ask for help at the first sign of a problem.

The team will have more time to do more valuable work and will shift from interrupt-driven support team to a team of peers developing one another.

Invest in Documentation

No team enjoys producing documentation but good documentation means answers are easier to find. Well-laid out, clear documentation can help colleagues help themselves rather than having to interrupt one another each time.

Good documentation, though admittedly painful to introduce, is a worthwhile investment and it pays for itself again and again in the future. It can be used as a reference by both new hires but also anyone else in the team whether they have been at the company for a while or not.

Conclusion

It can be very easy for the path of least resistance for the individual to become the path of most resistance for the organisation.

Encourage exploration and investigation among your team. Give them the tools and the practices so they can learn to fish themselves and the team will likely develop and grow even faster.
