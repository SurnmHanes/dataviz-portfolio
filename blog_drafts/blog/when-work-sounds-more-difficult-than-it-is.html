When Work Sounds More Difficult Than It Is

Standup Drift

In many software teams, daily standups start with a clear purpose. They allow the team to align quickly, identify blockers and share awareness. Over time however, they can drift away from this.

Updates become longer and more detailed. What was intended as a quick check-in can gradually turn into a narration or a status explanation.

I've noticed this in several teams I've worked in and I've probably been guilty of this myself too.

What Standup Is And Isn't

In Agile teams daily standups are meant to be for the members of the team to check in with one another. A very common way that standup is run is to address three key questions:

  1. What did you do yesterday?
  2. What are you planning to do today?
  3. Any blockers or concerns?

It should be a short sharp meeting to update all members of the team. It is called a standup as the participants traditionally stand, reinforcing the idea that it should be short and focused.

The goal is team clarity and alignment, not storytelling.

How Complexity Gets Communicated

Software and data work can be genuinely complex. Unexpected issues crop up, requirements change, systems behave in ways that we didn't anticipate.

But something subtle can happen in how we communicate that complexity. We can start to use broader, heavier language. Using phrases such as "technical challenges", "complex considerations", "unexpected issues" without always making the underlying problem(s) clearer.

Sometimes, work can feel like we're wrestling with something bigger than it really is. Not because the work isn't difficult, but because of how it's framed and communicated.

This isn't usually intentional but it is often the easiest way to describe something difficult in the moment.

Visibility vs Progress

In most organisations visibility matters. And work that is talked about, explained and described as difficult is far more noticeable and memorable than work that is simply completed and holds up quietly over time.

This difference in visibility can create an unintended dynamic where the way work is described can become as important as the work itself.

And when this starts to happen, the system rewards perceived effort more than outcomes.

How Teams Drift Here

This is never about individuals. It's usually about the team incentives.

When teams don't have clear ways to measure durability, simplicity or long-term quality, they often default to what is easiest to observe:

  • How busy someone sounds
  • How much they communicate
  • How much effort seems to be involved

Over time, this can subtly shift behaviour. Not for any malicious reason but rather because people tend to respond to what gets noticed and rewarded.

The Cost To The System

When this dynamic starts to take hold, the consequences can be three-fold:

  1. Decreased Clarity: The more abstract the language, the more difficult it is for others to understand what is actually going on.
  2. Complex-Sounding Problems: Problems start to sound more difficult than they need to. Which slows teams down over time.
  3. Outcome-Focused Work Less Visible: Even when it's the work that prevents issues from appearing in the first place.

A More Grounded Approach

A small shift can make a big difference. Instead of focusing on the effort involved, pivot the conversation around:

  • What problem are we solving?
  • What is the current state?
  • What is the next step?

Breaking the problem down into small logical steps like this encourages clear, simple explanations rather than detailed narratives.

What To Pay Attention To

Patterns matter more than individual moments. Everyone runs into difficulty sometimes. It's par for the course when working in software and data.

But when the same types of challenges appear repeatedly, it's worth stepping back and asking whether there's a deeper, structural issue at play.

Often, the biggest improvements don't come from working harder in the moment, but from reducing the number of problems that arise in the first place. In strong systems, the goal isn't to handle difficulty better, it's to design it out wherever possible.

Closing

In most teams, the real goal isn't to overcome difficulty, it's to reduce unnecessary difficulty altogether.

The best work often doesn't feel dramatic and can be quite bland. It's the kind that holds up quietly, long after it's been delivered.

And because of that, it can be so easy to overlook.
