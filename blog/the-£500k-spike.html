The £500K Spike

Introduction

The purpose of a spike in software development is to explore uncertainty. They exist to investigate a problem, evaluate possible approaches and return with a recommendation grounded in trade-offs.

Done well, a spike reduces risk. Done poorly, it can quietly introduce it.

One of the most common failure modes isn’t lack of effort, but lack of decision-ready thinking. What looks like thorough exploration on the surface can, in reality, defer the hardest part of the work: judgement.

This is how a spike designed to de-risk delivery can end up increasing cost, delay, and uncertainty.

When Spikes Fall Short

The Assignment

A typical spike begins with a simple goal: explore options for a new feature or technical approach and report back to the team.

The expectation is not just to surface possibilities, but to enable a decision.

The Response

Often, the output of these spikes appears strong at first glance. Multiple options are presented, sometimes with diagrams or examples, creating a clear sense of progress and activity.

But there is a recurring gap:

a gap in evaluation.

Options are listed, but not compared. Trade-offs are implied, but not explicit. There is too little emphasis on long-term maintenance, scalability or alignment with the existing system.

This isn’t typically due to lack of capability or effort. It is often a rational response to the environment. In teams where visibility, speed, and output are rewarded, producing multiple options signals productivity. Analysis, which is quieter and less visible, is easier to overlook.

The Decision Gap

From the outside, this kind of output can appear comprehensive. Without clearly defined evaluation criteria or explicit trade-offs, it becomes difficult to assess how decision-ready the work really is.

As a result, decisions tend to be guided by what is easiest to interpret: volume of output, clarity of presentation or intuitive appeal.

Decision-Making in a Vacuum

When options are not evaluated against shared criteria, teams are left without a solid basis for comparison.

In this vacuum, decisions naturally drift toward preference, familiarity, or the most compelling narrative. What “feels right” can start to outweigh what has been rigorously assessed.

The spike, intended to create clarity, ends up preserving ambiguity.

The Inevitable Rework

The consequences of this only become visible later. During refinement or implementation, the unanswered questions resurface:

  • What are the trade-offs between these options?
  • What is the effort required for each option?
  • Which approach is easiest to maintain?
  • How does this align with the broader system?

At this stage, the team is forced to revisit the analysis that should have been completed earlier. Work slows, confidence drops, and additional investigation is required.

The spike hasn’t removed uncertainty. Instead uncertainty has been deferred to a later point in time.

Why It Becomes Expensive

The cost of a weak spike is rarely isolated to the initial effort. It compounds across the delivery process:

Direct Costs

The salary for the spike and the effort of creating the subsequent story. The salaries for the refinement session and the duplication of these for the re-analysis.

Opportunity Costs

Time spent revisiting unresolved questions instead of progressing other valuable work.

Costs of Delay

The feature's launch will be delayed. This postpones the moment it can start generating value, solving a user pain point or capturing revenue. In a fast-moving market, the value of a feature is often tied to when it ships, not just what it is.

Trust Cost

If the feature was marketed or promised to (a subset of) customers, the delay will erode stakeholder / customer confidence in the team's ability to deliver.

Morale Cost

The cost of frustration for the team members who have to spend time covering the ground again and re-exploring the uncertainty.

The Antidote

A spike should aim to investigate unknowns and to return with a decision-ready recommendation, not just a set of possibilities.

A simple framework can help:

1. Define decision-making criteria upfront

What are we basing our recommendation on? Is it the time it takes to develop and test? Is it the cost of the proposed solution in terms of licensing perhaps? Is it how easy the solution is to maintain going forward? Or how easy it is to scale?

2. Evaluate each option against those criteria

A simple table showing the options on rows and the criteria on columns would suffice. (I tend to write out mine in paragraphs although I am trying to get better at the tabular approach.)

3. Provide a clear recommendation

Having evaluated all the options, a spike should include a clear, unambiguous recommendation that the team can take forward.

4. Make trade-offs explicit

No option will be perfect. A recommendation should be a fair assessment of the option chosen including its drawbacks.

This framework will only work in a culture where the leadership rewards rigorous thinking rather than long lists of options without clear evaluation.

Stop Performing, Start Deciding

Over time, if substance-light work becomes the norm, teams can end up building on sand. The emergencies that appear subsequently did not "come from nowhere". They are often predictable and in some cases avoidable.

One characteristic of a professional team is not in avoiding spikes. It's in conducting them in such a detailed, well-scoped, rigorous manner that it makes the subsequent work faster and more predictable.

Think of spikes as the building block for the development work that comes later. Your team's output is only as good as the quality of these investigations. A good amount of upfront effort will prevent any sand from shifting later in the development.

Invest in the craft of decision-making, and many of the emergencies that follow simply never happen.
