The Meeting Where Everyone Agrees

The Custom Visual

We built a report with a custom visual. It gave the page an interesting, striking and distinctive look. Something different from the native visuals that everyone else uses. Indeed we had comments from users on how "cool" the visual looked. It became a signature piece.

Then, as often happens over time, one custom visual became 6 on the same page. And users started complaining about the poor performance. The report took too long to load. We had to do something.

A spike was added to the sprint to look into options for optimising the custom visuals and report back accordingly.

The Investigation

The investigation wasn't given to a custom visual expert. It was also not assigned to a senior developer who had experience of custom visuals. Instead it was assigned to the next available developer. A well-intentioned decision driven by fairness rather than capability.

That developer did exactly as the system had trained them. They applied their native framework and since they were unfamiliar with custom visuals, their recommendation was simple: to remove them and replace with native visuals.

On the face of it, this was not a bad recommendation since performance of native visuals will be better and the performance complaint would stop.

Two caveats went unmentioned however: 1) no alternatives were explored and 2) the report would lose the one thing that made it distinctive.

The Missing Alternative

The system accepts this input as the only data point. In the interests of velocity and harmony, no counter-opinion is sought. There is no devil's advocate and the recommendation is treated as fact, rather than what it is: one person's perspective.

The spike was to investigate what could be done to optimise the custom visual. We solved a different problem: what could be done in place of the custom visual.

The reason for this is that the spike was handed to the wrong developer. There was no appreciation for experience or capability with custom visuals. So, although from their point, they did the best they could, they didn't know what they didn't know about custom visuals.

And the team compounds this by accepting their recommendation without challenge.

The Repeating Pattern

And this is now the pattern. A complex problem lands in the wrong hands. Not through malice but through a misguided sense of fairness.

That person does the best they can with the tools they have. They propose the simplest solution they can see. The team eager to move on, agrees.

Consensus. And the product gets a little dumber each time.

The team reinforce the narrative that it was the professional decision. It was the only sensible choice. And often the previous feature gets labelled as "unserious" in this narrative.

And the cycle repeats because the team learn that all complex solutions have simple, reductive solutions.

The problem-solving muscle atrophies because "why solve a hard problem when I can present an easy alternative?".

This is the Simple for Who paradigm in action: confusing "simple to build" with "simple to use".

For The Craftspeople

This is bad news for the true craftspeople in your team. They thrive on problem-solving and are happy to do the hard work others avoid to solve something difficult.

If the team learns to replace difficult with simple, the craftspeople become sidelined. Their problem-solving ability becomes less needed. Their robust, nuanced, slower solutions are made to feel like overkill and unnecessary.

For The Product

It's a slow death. Any innovation in the product is stripped away bit by bit. In its place are the basic least-offensive and easiest-to-build components. It goes from a high-quality product to one that struggles to wow users or solve complex problems elegantly.

And no one notices because every step was agreed upon by everyone in the room.

The Question

The next time you're in a meeting where everyone agrees, pause. Ask: "What aren't we considering? Who haven't we asked? Is this the only path?"

The most dangerous meetings aren't the ones where people argue. They're the ones where no one does.
