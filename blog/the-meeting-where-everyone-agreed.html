The Meeting Where Everyone Agrees

When Alignment Feels Easy

Agreement between people in a team is something that is strived for. It is a good thing for the team in general, it feels good for the individual and it is how the team make progress. It feels positive.

But in certain situations, agreement can actually be a bad thing.

How Context Shapes Outcomes

A common pattern that I've seen is a spike is allocated to a developer in the team. By definition a spike requires an open investigation to uncover understanding on an area that the team has no knowledge of.

The developer completes the investigation and reports back to the team with a handful of options and a recommendation.

Outcomes are rarely just a reflection of individual thinking. Instead they are heavily influenced by the context, constraints and information available at the time.

The organisation as a whole tends to favour an approach whether consciously or unconsciously. This culture then affects how individuals work. And it is within this context that a certain solution is recommended.

When Alternatives Stay Invisible

The developer proposes a simple solution to the issue. The team, influenced by the culture around them, are eager to move on and not knowing any different, agree with the proposal without much challenge.

To justify the decision, the team reinforce the idea it was the professional decision. It was the only sensible way forward.

Some alternative suggestions which may or may not have merit do not fit this context and so remain unvoiced and are not given due consideration. This means the best solution runs the risk of not being recommended.

Consensus as a Signal

In some teams, the recommendation is accepted without question, trusted implicitly and agreed as the way forward. Rather than as a viewpoint that the technical leads are allowed to poke holes in.

I've certainly had moments in the past where I've made recommendations based on my investigations but then subsequently someone more technical has identified a better solution.

The Subtle Drift Towards Simplicity

In teams, where there is no push back or questioning, the recommendation is accepted and the feature is replaced by something more straightforward. The product can become simpler over time as a result.

This cultural influence looms large. People are not malicious but will do more of what gets praise and acclaim. If a simple solution to a gnarly problem is celebrated without any discussion or questioning, a simple solution will most likely be suggested the next time as well.

The most skilled problem-solvers are not encouraged by this system to spend the requisite time to come up with better solutions. Instead their problem-solving ability is required less and their robust, nuanced solutions can be treated as overkill or unnecessary.

The Future of The Product

Since speed and quick decision making is highly rewarded in these cultures, complex solutions are not offered. And the product excellence erodes one spike at a time. Innovation is replaced by the least offensive and easiest to build components.

And the team doesn't notice because every step was agreed upon.

Healthy Tension

The next time you're in a meeting where there is little discussion and everyone seems to agree, ask: "What aren't we considering?", "Is this the only path?", "If we had infinite time, what does 'good' look like?"

The most dangerous meetings aren't where people argue. They're the ones where nobody does.
